When President John F. Kennedy announced in 1961 that America would land a man on the moon before the decade ended, he set in motion the most complex technological undertaking in human history.
The Apollo program required solving problems that had never been faced before.
Among the most daunting was navigation: how to guide a spacecraft a quarter million miles through empty space and land it safely on another world.
The solution was a small computer, the Apollo Guidance Computer, that changed the course of computing forever.

At the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, a team led by Charles Stark Draper accepted the impossible task.
They had to build a computer small enough to fit in the cramped command module and later the lunar lander.
It had to be tough enough to survive the violent shaking of a rocket launch.
It had to consume almost no power because every watt drained precious battery life.
And it had to be absolutely reliable because there would be no rescue mission if it failed.
The astronauts would be millions of miles from home, trusting their lives to this machine.

The engineers faced a fundamental problem: computers of that era filled entire rooms.
They used thousands of vacuum tubes and consumed enough electricity to power a small factory.
None of that would work in a spacecraft.
The team turned to a new technology: integrated circuits, or microchips.
These had only been invented a few years earlier and were still experimental.
Buying them was enormously expensive, but there was no alternative.
MIT ordered millions of them, effectively kickstarting the entire semiconductor industry.
Without Apollo, the modern microchip might have taken decades longer to develop.

The computer weighed seventy pounds and occupied about one cubic foot of space.
By today's standards, its specifications are almost laughable.
It had 72 kilobytes of memory, total, shared between permanent software and temporary data.
A modern smartphone has millions of times more capacity.
Its processor ran at slightly over one megahertz, a million operations per second.
A modern laptop runs thousands of times faster.
Yet this tiny machine had to perform calculations that would determine whether astronauts lived or died.

The software was written by a team of programmers at MIT, almost all of them young women.
They worked with pencil and paper, writing code that was then physically woven into rope memory.
Rope memory was a remarkable technology: wires were threaded through magnetic cores to store ones and zeros.
If a wire went through a core, that was a one.
If it went around, that was a zero.
Once manufactured, the memory could not be changed.
This meant the software had to be perfect before it was built.
There were no updates, no patches, no second chances.

The programmers became legendary for their attention to detail.
Margaret Hamilton, who led the software team, insisted on rigorous testing that others thought excessive.
She once took her young daughter to the lab, and the girl accidentally crashed a simulator by pressing the wrong keys.
Hamilton realized this could happen in space too, so she added error checking that would protect against such mistakes.
Her insistence on robustness saved the Apollo 11 mission months later.

On July 20, 1969, the lunar module Eagle descended toward the moon's surface.
Minutes before landing, the computer alarmed.
It was overloaded, receiving more data than it could handle.
The mission control center in Houston had seconds to decide whether to abort.
But Hamilton's software was designed for exactly this situation.
It prioritized landing tasks and ignored non-critical work.
The computer flashed a warning but kept flying.
Neil Armstrong took manual control and guided the lander safely to the surface.
Later analysis showed the software had performed perfectly.

The Apollo Guidance Computer pioneered concepts we now take for granted.
It was one of the first computers to use a real-time operating system, multitasking between different jobs.
It gave astronauts a simple display and keyboard, a user interface that let them interact directly with the machine.
It used software priorities to ensure critical tasks always came first.
Every modern aviation system, every medical device, every self-driving car relies on ideas first proven in that tiny lunar lander computer.

After Apollo, the technology trickled down into civilian life.
The microchips funded by NASA became the brains of calculators, watches, and eventually personal computers.
The software techniques developed by Hamilton and her team became standard practice in safety-critical systems.
The idea that computers could be small, reliable, and interactive transformed society.

Today we carry in our pockets more computing power than the entire Apollo program possessed.
Yet none of it would exist without that small computer that flew to the moon.
It proved that software could be trusted with human lives.
It showed that bold dreams could be realized with careful engineering.
And it reminded us that sometimes the smallest components make the biggest history.

The Apollo Guidance Computer sits today in museums, a modest-looking box covered in buttons and wires.
But inside it contains one of humanity's greatest achievements: not just landing on the moon, but proving that computers could take us anywhere.
