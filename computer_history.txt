Early humans used simple tools.
They counted on their fingers.
Abacuses appeared thousands of years ago.
These were early calculating devices.
Mechanical calculators came much later.
Blaise Pascal invented one in 1642.
It was called the Pascaline.
It could add and subtract numbers.
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz improved it.
His machine could also multiply and divide.
Charles Babbage designed an Analytical Engine.
This was in the 19th century.
It was a mechanical general-purpose computer.
He is often called the father of the computer.
Ada Lovelace wrote algorithms for it.
She is considered the first computer programmer.
Punched cards were used for input.
Herman Hollerith created a tabulating machine.
It used punched cards for the US Census.
This greatly sped up data processing.
The Jacquard loom also used punched cards.
It controlled weaving patterns.
The early 20th century saw electromechanical computers.
Konrad Zuse built the Z1 in Germany.
It was a programmable machine.
The Atanasoff-Berry Computer (ABC) was developed.
It was the first automatic electronic digital computer.
This was in the late 1930s.
World War II spurred computer development.
The Colossus computers were used by the British.
They helped break German codes.
The ENIAC was built in the United States.
It was the first general-purpose electronic digital computer.
It was massive and used vacuum tubes.
The first stored-program computer was the Manchester Baby.
It ran its first program in 1948.
The concept of stored programs was revolutionary.
John von Neumann was a key figure.
Transistors replaced bulky vacuum tubes.
This made computers smaller and faster.
The integrated circuit (IC) followed.
It put many transistors on one chip.
This led to microprocessors.
Microprocessors are the brain of a computer.
The first microprocessor was the Intel 4004.
It came out in 1971.
Personal computers began to emerge.
The Altair 8800 was an early hobbyist kit.
Apple Computer was founded in 1976.
The Apple II was a popular personal computer.
IBM introduced its personal computer in 1981.
This was the IBM PC.
It set an industry standard.
Microsoft Windows became dominant.
It provided a graphical user interface.
The internet connected computers globally.
ARPANET was an early precursor.
Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web.
This made information accessible.
Computers continue to evolve rapidly.
Moore's Law describes shrinking transistors.
Processing power increases exponentially.
New technologies like AI are emerging.
Quantum computing is on the horizon.
The history of computers is a story of innovation.
