Before computers became personal, they were room-sized monsters that only experts could touch.
In the 1950s and early 1960s, using a computer meant submitting a stack of punched cards to an operator and waiting hours or even days for results.
If your program had a single mistake, you received your cards back with an error message and started the entire process over again.
This batch processing system was efficient for the machine but miserable for the human.
Only a small number of trained specialists could truly work with computers, and students had almost no access at all.

At Dartmouth College in the early 1960s, two men decided this had to change.
John Kemeny and Thomas Kurtz were mathematicians who believed that computing was going to be fundamental to every field of study.
They argued that every Dartmouth student, whether studying art, history, or biology, should have hands-on access to a computer.
The problem was cost: a single computer cost millions of dollars, and only one person could use it at a time.
Their radical solution was time-sharing.

Time-sharing meant dividing the computer's processing power into tiny slices and rotating through multiple users so quickly that each person felt they had the machine to themselves.
While one user typed, the computer worked on another user's program for a fraction of a second, then switched back.
To the human, the response seemed instant.
Kemeny and Kurtz proposed building such a system on a General Electric GE-225 computer, a machine never designed for this purpose.
Most experts thought they were crazy.

Work began in 1963 with funding from the National Science Foundation.
The team faced enormous technical challenges.
They had to design not only the time-sharing operating system but also a programming language simple enough for beginners.
That language became BASIC, the Beginner's All-purpose Symbolic Instruction Code.
BASIC used plain English words like PRINT, LET, and IF, making it possible for someone with no technical background to write their first program in minutes.

On May 1, 1964, at 4:00 in the morning, the Dartmouth Time-Sharing System came to life.
Kemeny and Kurtz typed a simple program and watched as the computer responded instantly.
The miracle had worked.
Soon, terminals were placed all over campus: in libraries, dormitories, and student lounges.
Any student could sit down, type a few commands, and get an immediate response from the computer.
This was revolutionary.

The impact was immediate and profound.
Students in economics wrote programs to simulate markets.
Psychology students analyzed experiment data in real time.
Music students even programmed the computer to play simple melodies through a speaker.
Computing was no longer a mysterious ritual performed by priests in white lab coats.
It became a tool, like a library book or a laboratory instrument, available to anyone with curiosity and a problem to solve.

The Dartmouth system inspired similar projects at other universities and research centers.
It proved that computers could be social machines, serving many people at once rather than sitting idle between batch jobs.
This concept directly influenced the development of personal computers in the 1970s and 1980s.
When Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak built the Apple II, they were fulfilling the dream that Kemeny and Kurtz had started: a computer for the rest of us.

BASIC became the gateway language for an entire generation of programmers.
Countless engineers, scientists, and entrepreneurs wrote their first lines of code in BASIC on time-sharing systems or early home computers.
The language's simplicity lowered the barrier to entry and unleashed creativity on a massive scale.

Today, we take instant access to computing for granted.
Every time you open a laptop, tap a tablet, or connect to a cloud service, you are benefiting from the vision of Kemeny and Kurtz.
The Dartmouth Time-Sharing System was more than a technical achievement.
It was a philosophical statement that computing belongs to everyone, not just a privileged few.
That belief shaped the world we live in and continues to drive the democratization of technology across the globe.
