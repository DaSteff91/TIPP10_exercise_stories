In the history of computing, few machines are as foundational as the Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer, better known by its acronym, ENIAC.
Conceived in secret during the throes of World War II, its primary purpose was to calculate artillery firing tables for the United States Army.
These tables were critical for gunners to accurately aim their weapons, but the calculations involved were complex and time-consuming.
Before ENIAC, this work was done by human computers, predominantly women, using mechanical desk calculators.
A single trajectory could take hours to compute, and a full table required thousands of such calculations.
The war created an urgent need for a faster, more powerful method.

The project was born at the University of Pennsylvania's Moore School of Electrical Engineering.
The principal architects were John Mauchly, a physicist, and J. Presper Eckert, an engineer.
Their vision was radical: to build a machine that used vacuum tubes, not gears or mechanical relays, to perform its calculations at electronic speeds.
Construction began in secret in 1943, and the machine was completed in 1945.
By the time ENIAC was ready, however, the war in Europe was already over.
Its first major use was not for artillery tables, but for calculations related to the development of the hydrogen bomb, a testament to its immense potential.

ENIAC was a behemoth.
It weighed more than thirty tons and covered an area of about 1,800 square feet.
Its architecture consisted of forty panels, arranged in a U-shape, housing over 17,000 vacuum tubes, 70,000 resistors, 10,000 capacitors, and 6,000 switches.
The sheer number of vacuum tubes was a constant source of concern, as tubes were known to burn out frequently.
Legend has it that the machine would be non-functional about half the time due to a failed tube, though this is likely an exaggeration.
To keep it running, operators could not simply turn it off and on; the thermal stress of power cycling would damage the components.
Instead, some tubes were left glowing at a low power even when the machine was idle.

Programming the ENIAC was not like modern computing.
It had no stored programs in the way we understand them today.
To set up a new problem, a team of skilled technicians and mathematicians, a group of six brilliant women often overlooked in historical accounts, would have to physically reconfigure the machine.
This involved setting thousands of dials and switches and plugging and unplugging a complex web of cables to create the correct data pathways and program flow.
This process, known as "setting up the problem," could take days or even weeks.
Once programmed, however, ENIAC could perform calculations in seconds that would have taken days for a human.
It could execute up to 5,000 operations per second, a staggering speed for the time.

ENIAC was formally unveiled to the public in February 1946.
It was a sensation, demonstrating the awesome power of electronic computing to the world.
While it was not a stored-program computer—a concept articulated by John von Neumann shortly thereafter, where instructions and data are held together in memory—it was a crucial proof of concept.
It showed that large-scale electronic digital computing was not only possible but incredibly powerful.
The machine continued to operate until 1955, after which many of its components were dismantled and scrapped.
Today, parts of ENIAC reside in museums, serving as tangible relics of a pivotal moment in technological history, a direct ancestor to the devices we use every day.
Its legacy is not just in its hardware, but in the very idea of a general-purpose machine capable of tackling an endless array of complex problems at electronic speed.
