In the history of technology, few moments are as dramatic as the world's first public hack.
It happened not in the digital age, but in 1903, during a thrilling race to perfect wireless communication.
The main figure was Guglielmo Marconi, the Italian inventor who would later win a Nobel Prize for his work on radio.
Marconi had achieved something remarkable: sending messages through thin air without wires.
He was a showman as much as an inventor, and he planned a grand public demonstration in London to prove his system was not only impressive but also secure and private.

The Royal Institution, one of Britain's most prestigious scientific venues, invited Marconi to demonstrate his invention.
He was to send a wireless message from a station in Cornwall, nearly three hundred miles away, to the lecture hall in London.
The message would be received by a projector that flashed each letter onto a large screen for the audience to see.
Marconi boasted that his system was immune to interference, that messages could be sent privately without anyone eavesdropping or disrupting them.
This was the bold claim that attracted the attention of a man named Nevil Maskelyne.

Maskelyne was a stage magician, inventor, and rival with a deep interest in wireless technology.
He was also a skeptic who believed Marconi was overhyping his system's security.
Working from his own wireless station, Maskelyne decided to prove a point.
He planned to intercept and disrupt the transmission, demonstrating that Marconi's claims of privacy were dangerously false.

On the evening of June 4, 1903, the lecture hall was packed with scientists and journalists eager to witness the future.
Marconi's assistant began the transmission from Cornwall.
The projector in London flickered to life.
But instead of the expected scientific message, the audience saw something shocking.
Maskelyne had successfully intercepted the signal and overlaid his own.
Letter by letter, the projector spelled out a word that was decidedly unscientific: "RATS."
Then it continued with a taunting phrase: "There was a young fellow of Italy, who diddled the public quite prettily."

The audience gasped, then laughed.
Marconi was humiliated.
His demonstration was ruined, and his security claims were publicly shattered.
Maskelyne later admitted to the act, explaining that he wanted to expose the vulnerability of wireless communication before governments and businesses trusted it with sensitive information.
He argued that he was doing a public service by revealing the flaw before it could be exploited for more sinister purposes.

The event had profound implications.
Marconi and his company scrambled to improve security, eventually developing methods to encrypt messages and tune transmitters to specific frequencies.
But the fundamental lesson remained: any system that sends information through the air or across networks can be intercepted.
Maskelyne's hack was a precursor to every cybersecurity breach that would follow over the next century.

Today, we live with the consequences of that 1903 revelation.
Every email, every bank transaction, every private message travels through systems that must constantly defend against interception.
The cat-and-mouse game between security and exploitation began not in a computer lab, but in a London lecture hall, with a magician named Maskelyne proving that trust in technology must always be earned, never assumed.
His "RATS" message was the first hack, and it echoed through history as a warning we still struggle to heed.
