On August 25, 1991, a 21-year-old Finnish student posted a message to an internet newsgroup.
He said he was working on a free operating system, just as a hobby.
It would not be big and professional like the systems from Microsoft or IBM.
He asked if anyone was interested in his project or wanted to help.
That student was Linus Torvalds, and that hobby project would become Linux.

At the time, operating systems were expensive.
Unix was the standard for serious computing, but it cost thousands of dollars.
Microsoft Windows was becoming popular but was proprietary and locked down.
Students and hobbyists had few options.
Torvalds wanted something that could run on his personal computer, a machine with a modest Intel 386 processor.
He had been using Minix, a small educational Unix-like system, but wanted to build his own.

He started by writing a simple terminal emulator so he could connect to his university's computers.
Then he realized this could become something bigger.
He began building the core of an operating system, the kernel, that would manage hardware and run programs.
He announced his project and released the source code under a license that allowed anyone to use, modify, and share it for free.

The response was immediate and overwhelming.
Programmers around the world downloaded the code, tested it, fixed bugs, and added features.
Torvalds did not control the project in a traditional way.
He simply coordinated the flood of contributions pouring in from volunteers across the globe.
This was the birth of open source development on a massive scale.

What made Linux different was its philosophy.
Torvalds believed in technical merit above all else.
If your code worked and was well written, it would be accepted, regardless of who you were.
You did not need a degree, a job at a big company, or permission from anyone.
You just needed to contribute.
This attracted the best minds from every corner of the world.

By the late 1990s, Linux had become stable enough for serious use.
Companies began to notice.
IBM invested billions in Linux development.
Red Hat built a business selling support for the free system.
Nasa and other research institutions adopted it for their most demanding calculations.

The biggest breakthrough came with the rise of the internet.
When companies needed servers to run websites, they turned to Linux.
It was free, stable, and could run for years without crashing.
Apache, the most popular web server software, ran best on Linux.
Together, they powered the dot-com boom and every wave of innovation that followed.

Today, Linux is everywhere.
If you use an Android phone, you are using Linux.
If you visit Google or Amazon or Facebook, you are connecting to Linux servers.
If you watch Netflix or stream music, Linux handles those requests.
The world's top five hundred supercomputers all run Linux.
It controls stock exchanges, air traffic systems, and nuclear reactors.

Yet Linux remains free.
Anyone can download it, study it, and change it.
Thousands of developers continue to contribute, driven not by money but by the desire to build something useful together.
The source code is open for all to see, a guarantee that it will never be controlled by any single company or government.

Linus Torvalds still oversees the kernel, though he now works full time on Linux, supported by a foundation funded by the very companies that depend on his creation.
He never planned to change the world.
He just wanted a system he could use on his own computer.
But by sharing his work and inviting others to join, he started a revolution.

The story of Linux proves that collaboration can outperform competition.
It shows that giving things away can be more powerful than selling them.
And it reminds us that one person's hobby, shared generously with the world, can grow into something none of us could have built alone.
