In the summer of 1956, a small group of scientists gathered at Dartmouth College in Hanover, New Hampshire.
They came from universities and research labs, brought together by a young professor named John McCarthy.
McCarthy had convinced his colleagues that the time had come for a serious discussion about a radical idea.
He believed machines could be built to think, to learn, to use language, and to improve themselves.
He called this idea "artificial intelligence," a term he coined for the occasion.
The Dartmouth Summer Research Project lasted two months and involved only ten people.
But its impact would echo through the decades and shape the world we live in today.

The attendees read like a who's who of early computing.
Marvin Minsky was there, a brilliant MIT researcher who would become the face of AI for decades.
Claude Shannon attended, the father of information theory who had already revolutionized communications.
Herbert Simon and Allen Newell came, fresh from creating the first true AI program, the Logic Theorist.
These men were young, ambitious, and convinced they could solve the mystery of intelligence in a single summer.
They wrote proposals filled with optimism, predicting that machines would soon match human abilities.
Some thought it would take a generation at most.
None imagined it would take seventy years and counting.

The Logic Theorist, which Newell and Simon demonstrated at Dartmouth, was a genuine breakthrough.
It could prove mathematical theorems by manipulating symbols, mimicking human reasoning.
The program proved thirty-eight theorems from Whitehead and Russell's Principia Mathematica, a foundational text of mathematics.
For one theorem, it even found a proof more elegant than the one in the book.
This was the first demonstration that machines could do more than calculate numbers.
They could reason, think, and create.
The attendees left Dartmouth convinced that the future had arrived.

In the years that followed, funding poured into artificial intelligence research.
The US government, worried about the Soviet Union and the space race, saw potential in thinking machines.
Universities built labs, companies started projects, and optimism soared.
Minsky predicted that within a generation, the problem of creating AI would be substantially solved.
Simon claimed that machines would soon be capable of any work a man can do.
The public imagined robot servants and talking computers, just like in science fiction.
But reality was about to intervene.

The first setback came from the very nature of intelligence itself.
Early AI programs worked well in carefully controlled worlds with simple rules.
They could solve puzzles, prove theorems, and play games because these domains were small and logical.
But the real world was messy, ambiguous, and infinitely complex.
A program that could prove mathematical theorems could not understand a simple children's story.
A program that played perfect chess could not recognize a cat in a photograph.
The researchers had underestimated the problem by a wide margin.

By the early 1970s, the optimism had curdled into disappointment.
Government reports criticized AI for failing to deliver on its grand promises.
Funding dried up, labs closed, and the field entered what became known as the first AI winter.
Researchers learned to avoid the term artificial intelligence, calling their work robotics or expert systems instead.
But beneath the surface, work continued slowly, quietly, patiently.
They were gathering data, refining algorithms, and waiting for the world to catch up.

The waiting lasted decades.
Through the eighties and nineties, AI remained a backwater of computer science.
Expert systems found some commercial use in factories and hospitals, but they were brittle and limited.
Neural networks, an idea from the early days, were dismissed as outdated and ineffective.
Researchers who persisted were often marginalized, their papers rejected, their funding denied.
But a few stubborn souls kept working, convinced that the data would eventually arrive.
They were right.

The turning point came not from better algorithms but from more data.
The internet exploded in the late nineties, creating vast oceans of text, images, and video.
Companies like Google and Amazon began collecting information on an unprecedented scale.
Suddenly, the old algorithms that had seemed useless began to work.
Given enough examples, neural networks could learn patterns that had eluded them for decades.
A simple idea, hidden neurons adjusting their connections based on feedback, finally realized its potential.
The age of machine learning had begun.

In 2012, a team from the University of Toronto won a prestigious image recognition competition.
Their algorithm, a deep neural network, crushed the competition by a wide margin.
It learned to recognize objects by analyzing millions of examples, just as a child learns by seeing.
The result shocked the AI community and reignited interest in neural networks.
Within a few years, every major tech company had hired hundreds of researchers.
Deep learning became the hottest field in computer science, and the term AI returned to public discourse.

Today, machine learning is everywhere.
Your phone recognizes your face because neural networks learned from millions of faces.
Your email provider filters spam because algorithms learned what spam looks like.
Your streaming service recommends movies because systems learned your preferences.
When you speak to a virtual assistant, neural networks translate your words into commands.
When you search the internet, machine learning ranks the results.
When you translate a page, AI models that learned from billions of sentences do the work.

The technology that failed for fifty years now runs the world.
But the journey is far from over.
Today's AI is narrow, specialized, and fragile.
A system that masters chess cannot play checkers.
A model that writes fluent text cannot understand what it wrote.
The dream of general intelligence, machines that truly think like humans, remains as distant as it was in 1956.
John McCarthy's vision is half fulfilled, half waiting.

The ten men who gathered at Dartmouth that summer are almost all gone now.
They died without seeing their dream realized, without knowing whether their life's work mattered.
But their legacy grows stronger every day.
Every time you use a smartphone, every time you type a search, every time a machine understands your voice, you are proving them right.
They asked the right question, even if they could not find the answer.
And the answer, it turns out, was not a single breakthrough but a slow, grinding accumulation of data and patience.

In 1956, the first transistor radio had just appeared.
Computers filled entire rooms and cost millions.
The internet did not exist.
Yet those ten dreamers gathered in a small classroom in New Hampshire and dared to imagine machines that could think.
Seventy years later, you carry those machines in your pocket.
You talk to them, and they answer.
You ask them questions, and they respond.
The future they imagined is your present.
And somewhere, in a lab you will never visit, the next generation of dreamers is already imagining yours.
