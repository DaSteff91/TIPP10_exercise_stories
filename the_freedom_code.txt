In 1980, a printer jammed at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
This was not unusual. Printers jam all the time.
But this printer was different. It was a brand new Xerox 9700, the first laser printer many had ever seen.
It sat on the third floor, far away from the offices where the programmers worked.
When it jammed, nobody noticed until someone walked upstairs to check on their printout.
Hours of work could be wasted, waiting for pages that never came.

Richard Stallman worked in the AI lab at MIT.
He was one of the last true hackers, a programmer who believed software should be shared, studied, and improved.
For years, he had fixed the problems with the old printer by modifying its software.
The old printer would send a message to every user when it jammed.
No more wasted trips. No more wasted time.
But the new Xerox printer came with a secret.
Its software was locked. The source code was hidden.
Stallman asked the company for the code, just to fix the printer.
They refused. The code was their property, they said, and he could not have it.

This refusal changed everything.
Stallman sat in his office and watched his colleagues walk up and down the stairs, checking for paper jams.
He watched them curse the machine, curse the company, curse the wasted hours.
And he realized something fundamental had shifted in the world of computing.
When he started in the 1970s, software was shared like recipes.
If you fixed a bug, you gave the fix to everyone.
If you wrote something useful, you shared the code.
Now companies were locking everything down.
They were building walls around knowledge.
They were turning collaboration into crime.

Stallman did not get angry quietly.
He got angry strategically.
In September 1983, he posted a message to the net.unix-wizards newsgroup.
He announced a new project called GNU, a recursive acronym for "GNU's Not Unix."
He planned to build an entire operating system, every single piece, and give it away.
Not just the programs, but the source code.
Anyone could use it, study it, change it, share it.
He would create a world where no printer could ever trap its users again.

The task was insane.
Building an operating system required thousands of components, millions of lines of code.
Unix, the system he was replacing, had taken years and a team of Bell Labs engineers to build.
Stallman started alone, with no funding, no company, no guarantee of success.
He wrote a compiler called GCC so people could compile code.
He wrote a text editor called Emacs so people could write code.
He wrote debuggers, libraries, and tools, one by one, year after year.
Each piece was released under a license he created called the GNU General Public License.
This license had a clever twist, a trick Stallman called copyleft.
You could use the code, change it, even sell it.
But if you distributed it, you had to share your changes under the same terms.
The freedom could not be taken away. It was built into the legal DNA.

In 1985, Stallman founded the Free Software Foundation to support the work.
He published the GNU Manifesto, explaining his philosophy.
Software should be free, he argued, not in price but in freedom.
Free as in speech, not free as in beer.
Users deserved four essential freedoms: to run the program for any purpose, to study how it works and change it, to redistribute copies, and to distribute modified versions to the community.
These freedoms were not just convenient. They were moral.
Taking them away was, in his words, antisocial and unethical.

For years, the GNU project progressed steadily.
By 1991, Stallman had built almost everything needed for a complete operating system.
Almost everything. The kernel, the core that talks to the hardware, was still missing.
He had started one called Hurd, but development was slow.
Then a Finnish student named Linus Torvalds announced his own project.
He had written a kernel for his personal computer, just for fun.
He called it Linux and released it under the GPL, Stallman's license.
The combination was electric.
GNU plus Linux made the first complete free operating system.
You could install it on any PC and have a fully functional Unix-like system, entirely free, entirely open.

The world noticed.
Programmers loved having the code.
They fixed bugs, added features, and shared their work.
Companies noticed too.
Netscape, struggling against Microsoft, saw the power of this model.
In 1998, they released the source code for their browser, creating the Mozilla project.
Suddenly, open source was big business.

That same year, a group met in Palo Alto to rebrand the movement.
They worried that "free software" scared companies, who thought "free" meant "no money."
They wanted a term focused on the practical benefits, not the philosophy.
Christine Peterson suggested "open source."
Eric Raymond and Bruce Perens ran with it, founding the Open Source Initiative.
Their goal was the same software, but with a different message.
Free software was about freedom and ethics.
Open source was about better code, faster development, lower costs.

Stallman watched this with mixed feelings.
He welcomed the allies but worried the message was being diluted.
Freedom was not just a practical advantage, he argued.
It was a right. A moral issue. A social movement.
If you cared only about better code, you missed the point.
What happens when proprietary code is technically better?
What happens when your freedom is traded for convenience?
He continued to insist on the term "free software" and asked others to call the system GNU/Linux, not just Linux.
The naming debate became legendary, sometimes exhausting, but Stallman never wavered.
Principles mattered more than popularity.

The movement grew beyond anyone's expectations.
Today, free and open source software runs the world.
Linux powers most web servers, most supercomputers, most of the cloud.
Android, the world's most popular mobile operating system, is built on Linux.
The Apache web server, the MySQL database, the Python programming language, all open source.
Companies like Google, Facebook, Amazon, and Microsoft build their empires on code they did not write, code they can see, code they can change.
The printer that started it all is long forgotten, but its lesson lives on.

Stallman remains the conscience of the movement.
He travels the world giving talks, wearing his signature sandals, speaking with absolute certainty.
He is stubborn, uncompromising, and sometimes difficult.
But he is also the man who saw a locked printer and decided to build an entire universe of freedom in response.
He could have accepted the world as it was.
He could have shrugged and walked up the stairs like everyone else.
Instead, he wrote code, wrote licenses, and wrote a vision that changed everything.

The free and open source movement is not just about software anymore.
Its principles have spread to hardware, science, education, and culture.
Wikipedia, the encyclopedia anyone can edit, follows the same spirit.
Open access journals fight to make research available to all.
Creative Commons licenses let artists share their work with freedom.
The idea is simple and powerful: when we share knowledge, everyone benefits.
When we build walls, we all lose.

The next time you use Firefox, or Linux, or any open source tool, remember the printer.
Remember the jammed paper, the wasted walks, the refused request.
Remember one programmer who refused to accept a world where knowledge was locked away.
He started with a text editor and a compiler and a dream.
He ended up changing the world, one line of code at a time.
And it all began because a printer jammed, and someone decided that was not good enough.
