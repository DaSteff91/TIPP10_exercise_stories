In the early days of computing, there were no secrets.
Computers were room-sized machines locked in university basements.
Only a handful of researchers had access, and they all knew each other.
If you wanted to use the machine, you signed up for time, walked in, and ran your program.
There was no need for passwords because there was no one to hide from.
Then came the Compatible Time-Sharing System at MIT, and everything changed.

The year was 1961.
MIT had just built one of the first systems that allowed multiple people to use a computer at the same time.
Users sat at terminals connected by cables to a central mainframe.
They could type commands and get instant responses, a revolutionary experience.
But this created a new problem: how do you keep each user's files private?
If everyone shared the same machine, anyone could read anyone else's work.
The researchers needed a way to identify users and protect their data.

Fernando Corbató, a young MIT scientist, proposed a simple solution.
Give each user a secret code, a password, that they would type before starting.
The system would check the code and only grant access to the rightful owner.
It was a small idea with enormous consequences.
Corbató and his team implemented the first password system in 1961.
They thought they had solved the privacy problem forever.
They were wrong about the forever part.

The system worked well for a few months.
Researchers guarded their passwords, changed them occasionally, and trusted the machine.
Then one day, a graduate student named Allan Scherr found himself in a familiar situation.
He needed computer time for his research, but all the scheduled slots were full.
He could wait days for his turn, or he could find another way.
Scherr decided to test the security of the new password system.

He discovered that the computer stored all passwords in a single file.
If he could read that file, he could use anyone's account.
The file was supposed to be secret, protected by the system itself.
But Scherr noticed something interesting.
When the system printed error messages or diagnostic reports, it sometimes included parts of files.
He wrote a simple program that tricked the system into printing the entire password file.
Within hours, he had every password on the machine.

Scherr did not want to cause harm.
He just wanted computing time.
He logged into the busiest accounts during off hours and ran his research programs.
Nobody noticed at first.
The system kept running, the work got done, and no files were damaged.
But the experiment revealed a truth that would echo through the decades.
The very first password system, built with care by brilliant minds, was cracked almost immediately.

Corbató was disappointed but not surprised.
He knew that security was a game of cat and mouse.
You build a wall, someone finds a way over it.
You build a higher wall, they dig under it.
The goal was never perfect security, which was impossible.
The goal was making intrusion difficult enough that most people would not bother.
Scherr was not most people, and he had proven the system was vulnerable.

For years, the story remained inside MIT, a quiet lesson learned.
Passwords spread to other universities, then to companies, then to the public internet.
As computing grew, so did the importance of those little secret codes.
By the 1990s, anyone with an email account had a password.
By the 2000s, people had dozens of passwords, then hundreds.
The simple solution from 1961 had become a burden, a source of frustration and risk.

Today, security experts say passwords are broken.
People choose weak ones, reuse them everywhere, and fall for phishing scams.
Major breaches expose millions of passwords every year.
Companies try to replace them with fingerprints, face scans, and hardware tokens.
But passwords refuse to die.
They are too simple, too universal, too embedded in how systems work.

Fernando Corbató lived long enough to see his invention become a global nuisance.
He expressed regret, not for creating passwords, but for the misery they caused.
He imagined something better, something easier, something that would not require people to remember dozens of random strings.
But he also understood why passwords survived.
They required no special hardware, no biometric scanners, no additional cost.
Just a few characters typed on a keyboard, the same way they were typed in 1961.

Allan Scherr went on to a successful career in technology.
He never forgot the lesson of the stolen passwords.
Security is not about building impenetrable walls.
It is about understanding that humans are creative, persistent, and always looking for a way through.
The first hacker did not want to destroy the system.
He just wanted to get his work done.

Every time you type a password today, you are repeating a ritual that began in a basement at MIT.
Your fingers press the same keys that researchers pressed sixty years ago.
You are protecting files just as they protected theirs.
And somewhere, someone is probably trying to guess what you typed.
The game that Corbató started and Scherr exposed continues, every day, on every device.

Passwords were supposed to be temporary, a stopgap until something better arrived.
But temporary solutions have a way of becoming permanent.
We still trust our deepest secrets to short strings of characters.
We still forget them, reset them, curse them.
And we still have no perfect replacement.

The story of the first password is the story of computing itself.
Bold ideas, clever exploits, and the endless struggle between those who build walls and those who find ways through.
It began with one man trying to protect files and another trying to finish his thesis.
It continues with you, right now, typing your own secret code, hoping it is enough
